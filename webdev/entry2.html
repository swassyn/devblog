They’ve found that neural connections form and deepen most when we make mistakes doing difficult tasks rather than repeatedly having success with easy ones. What this means is that our intelligence is not fixed, and the best way that we can grow our intelligence is to embrace tasks where we might struggle and fail.”[5] “However, not everyone realizes this. Dr. Carol Dweck of Stanford University has been studying people’s mindsets towards learning for decades. She has found that most people adhere to one of two mindsets: fixed or growth. Fixed mindsets mistakenly believe that people are either smart or not, that intelligence is fixed by genes. People with growth mindsets correctly believe that capability and intelligence can be grown through effort, struggle and failure. Dweck found that those with a fixed mindset tended to focus their effort on tasks where they had a high likelihood of success and avoided tasks where they may have had to struggle, which limited their learning. People with a growth mindset, however, embraced challenges, and understood that tenacity and effort could change their learning outcomes. As you can imagine, this correlated with the latter group more actively pushing themselves and growing intellectually.”[5] I love the idea of viewing things as yet or not yet because as someone who grew up scared to fail because failure was met with retribution and sadness this is inspiring to me. TOP then goes on to talk about the pomodoro technique to help keep you renewed and refreshed while learning, and I am so happy to see this mentioned. I am not sure when or why I was introduced into this before, but it is something I use everyday, whether it be to accomplish chores, work, or learning new concepts. I highly recommend people give it a try and feel free to make changes to the “rules” of the technique and varying times. Below are my modifications: * I only do 25 minutes of work with 5 minute breaks. I don’t take long breaks currently. * Interruptions are okay if they are less than 1 min long and you constantly try to go back to the work at hand. I found the interruption reset to be far too punishing for me, especially at work, so as long as the effort for 25 minutes was there, then I earned that 5 minute break. * Create a list of things to work on and if you complete one, start work on the next item. Even if 1 minute is left. In that 1 minute you can set up and prepare so that when you come back from your break you can just jump right in. My preferred pomodoro site to use is : https://pomofocus.io/app “When things outside of your control happen you have to come up with a new plan that makes the most sense for that situation. You don’t have to blindly follow the rules. Come up with something that works for you. There’s no wrong answer here.“[9] This is something that I wish people like me would remember more. Don’t blindly follow the rules, it’s okay to experiment and change things up. There is no clear cut way to achieve something and things will come up outside your control and you will have to adjust to those roadblocks. That is okay and when they do, reassess the problem/goal at hand and create a new plan or implement modifications. The goal isn’t to do something the most efficiently in learning, the goal is to actually learn.
